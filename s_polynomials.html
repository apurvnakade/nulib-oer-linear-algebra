Section 0.5 Polynomials

Polynomials will play an important role in our treatment of linear algebra both in terms of building examples of vector spaces (3.3.20) and as tools for studying linear operators (5.5.27). We gather here some basic definitions and properties of polynomials. Although we will primarily be concerned with polynomials with real coefficients in this course, it is convenient to develop the theory over the complex numbers. As with Section 0.4, all theorems will be stated without proof.

Subsection 0.5.1 Basic definitions

Definition 0.5.1. Polynomials.

Assume \(X\subseteq \C\) and that \(Y=\R\) or \(Y=\C\text{.}\) A function \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) is called a polynomial if there is a nonnegative integer \(n\) and elements \(a_i\in \C\) such that

\begin{equation} f(x)=\anpoly\tag{0.5.1} \end{equation}

for all \(x\in X\text{.}\) We call the right-hand side of (0.5.1) a polynomial expression of \(f\text{.}\) For each \(1\leq i\leq n\) we call \(a_ix^i\) the \(i\)-th term of this polynomial expression, and \(a_i\) its \(i\)-th coefficient; we call \(a_0\) the constant term. Furthermore, if \(a_n\ne 0\text{,}\) then \(a_nx^{n}\) is called the leading term of the polynomial expression in (0.5.1), and \(a_{n}\) its leading coefficient.

A root of a polynomial \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) is an element \(c\in X\) satisfying \(f(c)=0\text{.}\)

A function \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) is called a real (resp. rational, resp. integer) polynomial if it has a polynomial expression as in (0.5.1) for which each coefficient \(a_i\) is real (resp. rational, resp. an integer).

Theorem 0.5.2. Basic properties of polynomials.

Assume \(X\subseteq \C\) and that \(Y=\R\) or \(Y=\C\text{.}\)

  1. If \(f\) and \(g\) are polynomials from \(X\) to \(Y\text{,}\) then so are \(f+g\text{,}\) \(fg\text{,}\) and \(cf\text{,}\) where \(c\) is any element of \(Y\text{.}\)

  2. If \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) is a polynomial and \(c\in X\) is a root of \(f(x)\text{,}\) then there is a polynomial \(g\colon X\rightarrow Y\) such that

    \begin{equation} f(x)=(x-c)g(x)\tag{0.5.2} \end{equation}

    for all \(x\in X\text{.}\)

  3. If \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) has a polynomial expression \(f(x)=\anpoly\) where \(a_n\ne 0\text{,}\) then \(f\) has at most \(n\) distinct roots in \(X\text{.}\)

As a result of the last property of Theorem 0.5.2 the expression (0.5.1) of a polynomial \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) is unique, as long as the domain \(X\) is infinite. This is made precise in the following corollary.

Corollary 0.5.3.

Assume \(X\) is an infinite subset of \(\C\) and that \(Y=\R\) or \(Y=\C\text{.}\) If \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) is a polynomial and \(X\) is infinite, then the expression \(f(x)=\anpoly\) is unique. More precisely:

  • If \(f\) is the zero function, then \(a_i=0\) for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\)

  • If \(f\) is not the zero function and we have two polynomial expressions

    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\anpoly=\bmpoly\text{,} \end{equation*}

    where \(a_n, b_m\ne 0\text{,}\) then \(n=m\) and \(a_i=b_i\) for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\)

Subsection 0.5.2 Degree of a polynomial

Once we know that the expression \(f(x)=\anpoly\) is unique, the various attributes of the polynomial expression (e.g., leading term, leading coefficient, etc.) can now be understood as properties of the function \(f\) itself. The following definition introduces once more such property, the degree of a polynomial. Note that the definition assumes we are in the setting stipulated in Corollary 0.5.3 to ensure uniqueness of the polynomial expression.

Definition 0.5.4. Degree of a polynomial.

Assume \(X\) is an infinite subset of \(\C\) and that \(Y=\R\) or \(Y=\C\text{.}\) Let \(f\colon X\rightarrow Y\) be a nonzero polynomial function, and let \(f(x)=\anpoly\) be its unique polynomial expression with \(a_n\ne 0\text{.}\) The degree of \(f\text{,}\) denoted \(\deg f\text{,}\) is defined as follows:

  • if \(f\) is the zero function, then we define \(\deg f=-\infty\text{;}\)

  • otherwise, we can write \(f(x)=\anpoly\) where \(a_n\ne 0\text{,}\) and we define \(\deg f=n\text{.}\)

We call the polynomial \(f\) linear if \(\deg f=1\text{,}\) quadratic if \(\deg f=2\text{,}\) and cubic if \(\deg f=3\text{.}\)

Defining the degree of the zero function to be \(-\infty\) may seem a little peculiar. Do not be disturbed! You can think of this as a convention that allows for clean statements of theorems like the following.

Theorem 0.5.5. Basic properties of degree.

Assume \(X\) is an infinite subset of \(\C\) and that \(Y=\R\) or \(Y=\C\text{.}\) Let \(f\) and \(g\) be polynomial functions from \(X\) to \(Y\text{.}\)

  1. \(\deg fg=\deg f+\deg g\text{.}\)

  2. \(\deg (f+g)\leq \max\{\deg f, \deg g\}\text{.}\)

Subsection 0.5.3 Factoring polynomials

Statement (2) of Theorem 0.5.2 makes a connection between roots of a polynomial and factorization; and the expression (0.5.2) can be thought of as a first step in writing the polynomial \(f\) as a product of linear polynomials. The existence of roots depends on the given domain of the function. Indeed, there are plenty of polynomials \(f\colon \R\rightarrow \R\) that have no roots whatsoever: for example, from the quadratic formula we know that any quadratic polynomial \(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c\) satisfying \(b^2-4ac\lt 0\) has no real roots. According to the fundamental theorem of algebra, the situation over \(\C\) is vastly different: not only does every polymonial have a root in \(\C\text{,}\) we can factor it completely as a product of linear polynomials. This is one of the main incentives for introducing the complex numbers as a number system.

Theorem 0.5.6. Fundamental theorem of algebra.

Any polynomial \(f(x)=\anpoly\text{,}\) thought of as a function from \(\C\) to itself, can be factored into linear terms as

\begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-z_1)(x-z_2)\cdots (x-z_n)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where the \(z_i\) are (not necessarily distinct) complex numbers.

Find a degree 3 polynomial whose coefficient of \(x^3\) equal to 1. The zeros of this polynomial are \(3\text{,}\) \(-4 i\text{,}\) and \(4 i\text{.}\) Simplify your answer so that it has only real numbers as coefficients.

Answer.
