Theorem 4.2.7. Calculating with orthogonal bases.

Let \((V, \langle , \rangle )\) be an inner product space. and let

  1. Let \(B=\{\boldv_1,\dots,\boldv_n\}\subseteq V\) be an orthogonal basis of \(V\text{.}\) For any \(\boldv\in V\) we have

    \begin{equation*} \boldv=c_1\boldv_1+c_2\boldb_2+\cdots +c_n\boldv_n\text{,} \end{equation*}

    where

    \begin{equation*} c_i=\frac{\langle \boldv,\boldv_i\rangle}{\langle\boldv_i,\boldv_i\rangle} \end{equation*}

    for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\) If \(B\) is orthonormal, so that \(\langle \boldv_i, \boldv_i\rangle =1\) for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{,}\) then \(c_i=\langle \boldv, \boldv_i\rangle\text{.}\)

  2. Generalized Pythagorean theorem.

    Let \(B=\{\boldv_1,\dots,\boldv_n\}\subseteq V\) be an orthonormal basis of \(V\text{.}\) Given \(\boldv=c_1\boldv_1+c_2\boldb_2+\cdots +c_n\boldv_n \text{,}\) we have

    \begin{equation*} \norm{\boldv}=\sqrt{c_1^2+c_2^2+\cdots c_n^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
