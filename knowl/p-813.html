The following argument is a common invalid proof of the reverse implication:

  1. Assume \(AB\) is invertible.

  2. Then \(AB\) is invertible.

  3. Then the inverse of \(AB\) is \(B^{-1}A^{-1}\text{.}\)

  4. Then \(A^{-1}\) and \(B^{-1}\) exist. Hence \(A\) and \(B\) are invertible.

Where is the flaw in our logic here? The second statement only allows us to conclude that there is some mystery matrix \(C\) satisfying \((AB)C=C(AB)=I\text{.}\) We cannot yet say that \(C=B^{-1}A^{-1}\text{,}\) as this formula from Theorem 2.3.6 only applies when we already know that \(A\) and \(B\) are both invertible. But this is exactly what we are trying to prove! As such we are guilty here of “begging the question”, or petitio principii in Latin.
