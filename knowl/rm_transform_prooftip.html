Remark 4.2.7. The one-step linear transformation proof.

As a sort of converse to (3) in Theorem 4.2.5, observe that if \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) satisfies

\begin{equation*} T(c\boldv_1+d\boldv_2)=cT(\boldv_1)+dT(\boldv_2) \end{equation*}

for all \(c, d\in \R\) and \(\boldv_1,\boldv_2\in V\text{,}\) and \(\boldw\in W\text{,}\) then \(T\) is linear. Indeed, taking the special case \(c=d=1\) yields (1) of Definition 4.2.1; and choosing \(d=0\) yields (2) of Definition 4.2.1.

This observation gives rise to an alternate one-step technique for proving linearity: given \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\text{,}\) show that

\begin{equation*} T(c\boldv_1+d\boldv_2)=cT(\boldv_1)+dT(\boldv_2) \end{equation*}

for all \(c, d\in \R\) and \(\boldv_1,\boldv_2\in V\text{.}\)
