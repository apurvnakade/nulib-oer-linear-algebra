Statement (2) of TheoremÂ 0.5.2 makes a connection between roots of a polynomial and factorization; and the expression (0.5.2) can be thought of as a first step in writing the polynomial \(f\) as a product of linear polynomials. The existence of roots depends on the given domain of the function. Indeed, there are plenty of polynomials \(f\colon \R\rightarrow \R\) that have no roots whatsoever: for example, from the quadratic formula we know that any quadratic polynomial \(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c\) satisfying \(b^2-4ac\lt 0\) has no real roots. According to the fundamental theorem of algebra, the situation over \(\C\) is vastly different: not only does every polymonial have a root in \(\C\text{,}\) we can factor it completely as a product of linear polynomials. This is one of the main incentives for introducing the complex numbers as a number system.
